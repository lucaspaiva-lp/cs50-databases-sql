## Report — SQL Moneyball Module Reflection

Throughout the development of the activities in this module, it became evident that most of the difficulties were not related solely to writing SQL itself, but mainly to the precise interpretation of the problem statements and the correct translation of requirements into consistent queries. In several moments, seemingly simple terms carried direct technical implications regarding the expected output format, as seen in the understanding of expressions such as Single Column and in the explicit definition of which tables should be used to compose the answer.

A recurring difficulty was the careful reading of the complete requirements, especially when examples provided in the problem statement described more precisely the behavior expected by the automatic grader. Initially, these examples were interpreted merely as illustrations, when in practice they functioned as a detailed specification of ordering, grouping, and the correct alignment of data across different tables. From this realization, it became clear that strictly following these examples was essential for the result to be validated correctly.

From a technical standpoint, the module required significant maturation in the use of JOINs across multiple tables, particularly in the correct alignment of records that share more than one logical criterion, such as player_id and year. The need to ensure that salaries and performances referred to exactly the same year highlighted the importance of additional conditions in joins, going beyond simple primary key and foreign key associations.

Another aspect that caused difficulty was the correct use of aggregations and ordering, especially the distinction between ordering by raw column values and ordering by values derived from calculations. At a certain point, it became necessary to understand that ORDER BY can operate directly on mathematical expressions, which made it possible to calculate metrics such as cost per hit or cost per RBI without the need to materialize these values beforehand in separate subqueries.

Subqueries, in turn, represented one of the most challenging aspects. There was initial difficulty in correctly defining which column should be returned, in which contexts operators such as IN should be used, and how to structure nested queries without violating SQL rules regarding scalar returns versus result sets. This process required more time for reasoning and experimentation, but it contributed directly to a stronger understanding of query scope, progressive filtering, and set intersection.

Overall, although some questions presented a significantly higher level of complexity compared to earlier ones—especially those involving combined calculations, conditional ordering, and multiple subqueries—the process was highly productive. The difficulties encountered revealed specific areas that still require further review, but they also marked clear progress in the ability to interpret complex requirements and translate them into correct and efficient SQL queries. This module, in particular, served as an important milestone in the development of logical reasoning applied to relational databases and will certainly be revisited to consolidate the more advanced concepts presented.
